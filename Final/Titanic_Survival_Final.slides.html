K Nearest Neighbors¶

This classifier is pretty cool.

It essentially relies on the idea that points in an Nth-dimensional space that fall closest to your point of interest will most likely predict the class for that point.

The K refers to how many neighbors' classes you want to assess to make your classification.

Because this classifier uses euclidean distance to determine proximity to your point of interest, it's important that all axes/features be normalized to the same scale.

If you have a feature on a scale that is many orders of magnitude larger than another dimension, the distances will be unreasonably high in that axis. Normalizing so that all feature axes are on a 0.0-to-1.0 scale eliminates this completely.

So for a k of 3, the data might look like this:

You can optionally weight the class instances for the neighbors by dividing the class by the distance (with the idea that the nearer a neighbor is, the more likely your point is to be similar to it in class).

K can be adjusted as needed, but should generally be odd (so as to never have ties for class).

For classifiers: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1¶

One of the most important ways of telling whether your classifiers are doing a good job is to measure accuracy, precision, and recall. These essentially tell us how well your classifier predicted class A when class A was supposed to be predicted and never else.

Note:

$TP$ = True Positive, $TN$ = True Negative, $FP$ = False Positive, $FN$ = False Negative

Accuracy¶

Accuracy can be thought of as being the proportion of times your classifier correctly classified a sample. The equation is as follows:

$Accuracy = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + FP + TN + FN}$¶

This alone doesn't give you a sense of what the $FP$ is. It's possible that in order to classify $TP$ correctly, you your classifier is being a little too generous, and you'll have a high $FP$ rate because of it.

Precision¶

Precision starts getting more specific. Precision says, of all the times your classifier predicted a positive, how often was it right? The equation looks like this:

$Precision = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$¶

This still isn't enough to get a sense of whether your classifier is doing a good job, though, because it's possible your classifier is too conservative, and almost never predicts a positive unless it's at an absolute extreme. You may have a very high $FN$ rate here you that don't see

Recall¶

Recall breaks this problem down a little bit more. Recall asks: of all the positives in the dataset, how many did the classifier actually get? The equation looks like this:

$Recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$¶

The two of these together give you a sense of how well your classifier is doing. So we'll be looking at both.
